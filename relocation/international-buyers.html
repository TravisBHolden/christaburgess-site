Frequently Asked

Your Questions, Answered

✦ Can non-US citizens buy property in Texas?

Yes. There is no citizenship requirement to purchase real estate in Texas. Non-US citizens — including foreign nationals on visitor visas, work visas, or no visa at all — can purchase residential and investment property. The purchase process is the same as for US citizens from a contract and title perspective. The differences appear in financing, tax obligations, and ongoing reporting requirements.

✦ Do I need a visa or green card to buy?

No. There is no visa or immigration status requirement to purchase real estate in the United States. You do not need a green card, work authorization, or any specific visa status to buy a home in Texas. Purchasing property does not create any immigration benefit — it does not grant a visa, green card, or any right of residency. Immigration status and property ownership are entirely separate legal matters.

✦ What is FIRPTA and how does it affect me?

FIRPTA (Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act) is a US tax withholding requirement that applies when a foreign person sells US real property. When a foreign seller sells, the buyer is required to withhold 15% of the gross sales price and remit it to the IRS as a prepayment of the seller's potential capital gains tax.

If you are buying from a US citizen seller: FIRPTA does not affect you. The withholding obligation applies to the seller, not you.

If you are a foreign national and eventually sell: Your buyer will be required to withhold 15% of the gross price at closing and remit to the IRS. This is not a final tax — it's a withholding. You file a US tax return, report the actual gain, and any overpayment of withholding is refunded. Working with a CPA familiar with FIRPTA is essential when you sell.

✦ Can foreign nationals get a mortgage in Texas?

Yes — with a specialized loan product. Foreign national mortgage programs exist at several US lenders and are specifically designed for buyers without US Social Security numbers or established US credit history. Key parameters:

  • Down payment: Typically 25–40% of purchase price
  • Documentation: Foreign bank statements (12–24 months), employer letters, income verification from your home country
  • ITIN loans: For buyers with an Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN) but no Social Security Number — available from select lenders
  • Asset verification: Many programs rely heavily on asset documentation in lieu of US credit history
  • Interest rates: Generally slightly higher than conventional rates, reflecting the additional documentation complexity

Many international buyers purchase with cash, which simplifies the process significantly. Cash purchases remove the mortgage contingency and can accelerate closing timelines.

✦ How do I handle currency exchange and international wire transfers?

All US real estate transactions are conducted in US Dollars (USD). For international buyers, this means converting funds before wiring to the title company. Key guidance:

  • Currency brokers: Use Wise, OFX, or a dedicated currency broker rather than your bank for conversion — rates are significantly better on large transactions. On a $500,000 purchase, the rate difference can be thousands of dollars
  • Wire timing: Allow 2–5 business days for international wires to arrive and clear. Wire two to three days early to avoid closing delays
  • Wire fraud is a real risk: Always verify wire instructions by phone call to the title company using a number you look up independently — not a number provided in an email. Wire fraud in real estate is sophisticated; criminals intercept email chains and substitute fraudulent wire instructions. One phone call verification prevents this entirely
  • Earnest money: The initial earnest money deposit (typically 1% of purchase price) must arrive within 3 business days of contract execution — plan your currency conversion accordingly

✦ Can I buy as an LLC or foreign corporate entity?

Yes. Foreign nationals can purchase Texas real estate through a US LLC, foreign LLC, or other corporate entity structures. This approach is used by many international investors for asset protection and estate planning purposes. However, the tax implications of entity ownership are complex — ownership structure affects FIRPTA withholding calculations, ongoing income tax reporting, and estate tax exposure at the federal level. Work with a US tax attorney experienced in cross-border real estate ownership structures before choosing your purchase entity. The right structure depends on your country of residence, intended use of the property (primary residence vs. investment), and long-term plans.

✦ What is title insurance and do I need it?

Title insurance is a one-time premium paid at closing that protects against defects in the ownership history of the property — undisclosed liens, prior ownership disputes, errors in public records, forged deeds, and other issues that could challenge your right to own the property after purchase.

Title insurance is standard in US real estate transactions and is effectively required in any transaction involving a mortgage (the lender requires their own policy). Even cash buyers should purchase an owner's policy — the cost is a fraction of the purchase price and provides permanent protection.

In Texas, title companies handle the closing process — they receive earnest money, conduct the title search, issue the title commitment, hold funds in escrow, and coordinate the final closing. They are neutral parties serving both buyer and seller in the transaction.

✦ How does the Texas closing process work?

Texas uses a title company-centered closing process. Here is the typical timeline:

  • Executed contract: Both parties sign. The clock starts
  • Earnest money: Buyer wires earnest money to the title company within 3 business days
  • Option period: Typically 7–10 days during which buyer can back out for any reason for a small fee (option money, $100–500). Used for inspections
  • Title commitment: Title company issues a commitment showing the property's title history and any requirements to clear title
  • Loan approval: If financing, final approval typically required 7–10 days before closing
  • Final walkthrough: 24 hours before closing, buyer confirms property condition
  • Closing day: Sign documents, wire remaining funds (down payment + closing costs), title company disburses funds to seller
  • Funding and keys: Texas is a "dry closing" state — keys typically transfer after the title company confirms the wire has funded

Remote closing: International buyers can close remotely via DocuSign and remote notarization. This is well-established in Texas and does not require physical presence at the title company.

✦ What are the ongoing ownership costs?

  • Property taxes: 2.3–3.2% of assessed value annually in the Katy-Fulshear area — paid in arrears, due by January 31 each year. This is the largest ongoing cost. On a $500,000 home, budget $12,000–$16,000/year
  • Homeowner's insurance: $2,000–$5,000+ per year depending on coverage, flood zone, and home value
  • HOA dues: $700–$1,500/year is typical for Katy master-planned communities. Some communities have multiple HOA layers
  • Utilities: $200–$500+/month depending on home size and season — summer electricity bills in Texas can be significant
  • Maintenance reserve: Budget 1–2% of home value per year for ongoing maintenance and repairs
  • Property management: If renting the property, professional management typically runs 8–12% of monthly rent

✦ Can I rent the property?

Long-term leases (12+ months) are typically permitted in Katy master-planned communities. Most communities, however, explicitly prohibit short-term rentals (Airbnb, VRBO, stays under 30 days). Review the HOA's Declaration of Covenants before purchase if rental income is part of your investment thesis.

Tax implications: Rental income from US property is taxable US income regardless of where you live. Non-resident alien landlords file Form 1040-NR. You may elect to be taxed as "effectively connected income" which allows you to deduct expenses (mortgage interest, property taxes, depreciation, management fees) against rental income — often the more tax-efficient treatment. A US CPA experienced in non-resident returns is essential.

The Katy area has strong rental demand driven by Energy Corridor relocations, temporary corporate assignments, and families entering the area before purchasing. A well-maintained home in Cinco Ranch or Cross Creek Ranch typically rents within 30–45 days of listing.

✦ Which communities are most popular with international buyers?

The Katy area is one of the most diverse suburban communities in the United States. South Asian, East Asian, Latin American, and European expatriate populations are well-established across the area. The communities that have the strongest international buyer presence:

  • Cinco Ranch: Largest established international community presence. Strong South Asian and East Asian community networks. Excellent Katy ISD schools
  • Cross Creek Ranch (Katy ISD sections): Growing international buyer community. School district verification critical — Katy ISD sections are preferred by many families
  • Grand Lakes: International professionals working in the Energy Corridor

Cultural amenities — international grocery stores, religious institutions representing many faiths, cultural associations — are well-represented in the Katy area. This is not a community where international families feel isolated; it's one where they find established networks from day one.

✦ Can my children enroll in Katy ISD schools?

Yes. All children in the United States are entitled to a free public school education, regardless of immigration status, citizenship, or documentation. This is settled federal law under Plyler v. Doe (1982) and no school district can legally deny enrollment based on immigration status.

Bring translated academic records, immunization records, and proof of residency (your lease or utility bill at your Katy address) to enrollment. ESL programs are available at most Katy ISD campuses. Families arriving with children enrolled in IB programs should ask specifically about international transcript equivalency procedures at the high school level.

✦ What does CIPS actually mean for the buying experience?

Working with a CIPS-certified agent means working with someone who has specifically trained in the unique challenges of cross-border transactions — not just someone who has worked with international buyers by chance.

In practice, for international buyers this means:

  • Understanding which questions require a US attorney vs. which are handled in the real estate transaction itself
  • Coordinating with lenders who have foreign national mortgage programs
  • Guiding wire transfer timing and fraud prevention
  • Organizing remote closings via DocuSign and remote notarization
  • Communicating in Spanish fluently for Spanish-speaking buyers — not through translation, in the actual language
  • Connecting buyers with the right professional network: US tax attorneys, CPAs, immigration attorneys when needed, property management firms for investment buyers

The transaction itself is standard Texas real estate. The complexity for international buyers sits in the surrounding professional and financial infrastructure. Having a CIPS agent means having someone who has navigated that infrastructure many times before.
