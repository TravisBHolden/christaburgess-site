Can non-US citizens buy property in Texas?

Yes. There is no citizenship requirement to purchase real estate in Texas or the United States. Non-citizens — including those on work visas, student visas, or with no US immigration status at all — may legally purchase residential property. Foreign nationals, foreign corporations, and foreign trusts may all hold US real estate.

The process has additional steps compared to a domestic purchase (tax documentation, financing considerations, etc.), but the fundamental right to buy exists regardless of citizenship or immigration status.

Do I need a visa or green card to buy property in the US?

No visa or green card is required to purchase property. You do not need to be a US resident or have any immigration status to buy real estate.

That said, your immigration status affects other things: your ability to obtain a US mortgage, your tax filing obligations, and your ability to physically be in the United States to manage the property. Ownership of US real estate does not, by itself, grant you any immigration status or right to remain in the US.

What is FIRPTA and how does it affect me?

FIRPTA stands for the Foreign Investment in Real Property Tax Act — a US federal law that requires buyers to withhold a percentage of the purchase price when buying from a foreign seller, and remit it to the IRS. The purpose is to ensure that foreign sellers pay US capital gains tax on real estate profits.

  • If you're a foreign buyer purchasing from an American seller: FIRPTA does not directly affect you.
  • If you're a foreign seller in the future: Your buyer will be required to withhold 15% of the gross sales price and send it to the IRS. You can later file a US tax return and receive a refund of excess withholding if your actual gain is lower.
  • If you're purchasing from a foreign seller: I flag this early — it affects closing logistics and timeline. This is one of many areas where my CIPS training makes a practical difference.

Can foreign nationals get a mortgage in the United States?

Yes, but it requires more work than a standard domestic purchase. Options for foreign nationals include:

  • Foreign National Loan Programs: Several US lenders offer portfolio mortgage products specifically designed for non-resident foreign buyers. These typically require 25–40% down payment, proof of income from the home country, bank statements, and sometimes an employer letter.
  • ITIN Loans: If you have a US Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN) but no Social Security Number, some lenders will underwrite based on your ITIN and income documentation.
  • Cash Purchases: Many of my international clients purchase with cash, which eliminates the financing complexity entirely and often makes offers more competitive.

I don't have a US credit history. How do lenders evaluate me?

US credit scores (FICO) are built from US credit history. If you're new to the US, you have no score. Lenders who work with foreign nationals handle this through:

  • Alternative credit documentation — Foreign bank statements, employer letters, overseas credit reports (some lenders accept these), and evidence of consistent payment history
  • Higher down payments — A larger down payment (30–40%) reduces lender risk and compensates for lack of credit history
  • Asset verification — Demonstrating substantial liquid assets is often weighted heavily
  • Co-borrowers — In some cases, a US-based co-borrower with established credit helps

What should I know about currency exchange and wire transfers?

All US real estate transactions are conducted in US dollars. If your funds are held abroad, you'll need to convert and wire them to the US title company.

  • Use a reputable foreign exchange service rather than your home bank for large conversions — the rate spread on bank currency conversion can be significant on a $400,000+ transaction. Services like Wise, OFX, or currency brokers often provide better rates.
  • Wire fraud is a serious risk — before wiring any funds, verify wire instructions directly with the title company by phone using a number you independently look up. Never rely solely on emailed wire instructions. This is a common fraud vector in real estate.
  • Allow time — international wires can take 2–5 business days. Plan closing timelines accordingly.
  • Anti-money laundering requirements — US title companies are required to verify the source of funds for cash transactions. Be prepared to provide documentation of where your money came from.

Can I buy US property as an LLC or foreign entity?

Yes. Foreign corporations, LLCs, partnerships, and trusts can own US real estate. Many international investors prefer to purchase through a US LLC or trust structure for liability protection, estate planning purposes, or tax efficiency.

Tax implications are complex — FIRPTA, estate tax exposure for foreign nationals, and income tax treatment of rental revenue all interact in ways that require professional advice. I strongly recommend working with a US tax attorney or CPA experienced in foreign national real estate before structuring the purchase. The structure you use affects your tax liability for decades. What is title insurance and why do I need it?

Title insurance is standard in US real estate transactions and protects the owner and lender against defects in the property's ownership history — things like undisclosed liens, prior ownership disputes, errors in public records, or fraud in the chain of title.

In Texas, the title company conducts a title search, issues a title commitment showing the state of title, and then issues a title insurance policy at closing. The one-time premium is paid at closing. For international buyers who may not be familiar with US property records or local history, title insurance is not optional — it's your protection against risks you cannot independently verify from abroad.

How does the Texas closing process work for international buyers?

Texas closings are handled by title companies — not courts or attorneys in most transactions. The typical flow:

  1. Executed contract — signed by buyer and seller (DocuSign works internationally)
  2. Earnest money — wired to the title company within a few days of contract execution
  3. Option period — typically 7–10 days for inspections and due diligence; you can terminate for any reason
  4. Loan processing (if financing) or funds verification (if cash)
  5. Title commitment issued — title company reviews ownership history
  6. Closing day — documents signed; can be done in-person or via remote notarization
  7. Funding and keys — once all documents are executed and funds received, the deed records and keys transfer

Remote closings are common and well-managed by experienced Texas title companies. I coordinate closely with the title team to make sure the international logistics work.

What are the ongoing costs of owning property as a foreign buyer?

  • Property taxes: 2.3%–3.2% of assessed value annually in the Katy area
  • Homeowner's Insurance: Typically $2,000–$5,000+/year depending on home value. Flood insurance may be required or advisable separately.
  • HOA dues: $700–$1,500/year in most Katy-area master-planned communities
  • Utilities: $200–$500+/month depending on home size and season
  • Maintenance: Budget 1–2% of home value annually for routine upkeep
  • Property management (if renting): Typically 8–12% of monthly rent
  • US tax filing: If you earn rental income or eventually sell, US federal tax filings are required

Can I rent the property if I'm not living here full-time?

Yes. Many international buyers purchase investment properties or homes they plan to use seasonally and rent when they're abroad. Key points:

  • HOA rules may restrict short-term rentals — Most Katy-area master-planned communities do not allow short-term rentals (Airbnb/VRBO). Long-term leases (12+ months) are typically permitted.
  • Property management: A local company handles tenant screening, leasing, maintenance, and rent collection. Fees typically run 8–12% of monthly rent.
  • US rental income is taxable — You'll need to file a US federal tax return. Work with a CPA experienced in foreign national taxation. I can refer you to the right person.

Which communities are most popular with international buyers?

In my experience, Cinco Ranch, Cross Creek Ranch (Katy ISD sections), and Grand Lakes are the most common destinations — particularly for families prioritizing Katy ISD schools. These communities have well-established South Asian, East Asian, and Latin American populations, making the transition meaningfully easier.

For buyers prioritizing newer construction or larger homes, Fulshear-area communities are also popular. Buyers seeking privacy or luxury sometimes look at gated sections within Cinco Ranch.

The Katy area's international population is large, active, and genuinely welcoming. International grocery stores, culturally diverse restaurants, international-focused faith communities, and established immigrant networks make the transition meaningfully easier here than in many US metro areas.

What do I need to know about school enrollment for international children?

Public school enrollment in Texas is open to all children regardless of immigration status — this is established federal law (Plyler v. Doe). Katy ISD enrolls students based on proof of residency within the district, not based on citizenship or visa status.

  • Bring translated copies of academic records — school counselors use these for grade placement
  • ESL (English as a Second Language) programs are available at most Katy ISD campuses
  • International Baccalaureate (IB) and advanced academic programs are available at certain campuses for high-achieving students
