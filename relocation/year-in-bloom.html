A Personal Chapter

From Christa

Most relocation guides skip the yard. I can't. I have a Texas A&M Horticulture degree, spent years running a commercial landscape design, installation, and maintenance firm in Houston, and went abroad to New Zealand through the International Agricultural Exchange Association to study agricultural systems. The land is how I see places. It's how I learned to understand them.

When I walk a property with a buyer, I'm reading the landscape as much as the structure. The orientation of the lot. The age of the tree canopy. Whether the drainage flows toward or away from the foundation. What's planted in the beds and whether it will survive a Houston summer.

This chapter is for people who want to understand the land they're moving onto — not just the house sitting on top of it.

The Climate

Gulf Coast Growing Zone

Katy and Fulshear sit in USDA Hardiness Zones 9a/9b — the Gulf Coast climate zone that defines subtropical Houston. What this means practically: hot, humid summers that run from May through September; mild winters that rarely drop below 28°F; and a growing season that, with the right plant selection, runs essentially year-round.

The challenge isn't cold — it's heat. Plants that succeed in Dallas don't always translate to Houston. The combination of high temperature, high humidity, and heavy clay soils creates a specific set of conditions that rewards plants adapted to those conditions and punishes everything else.

The good news: the Gulf Coast region has a remarkable native plant palette, and the number of beautiful, low-maintenance plants adapted to these exact conditions is larger than most gardeners realize when they arrive.

The Calendar

Your Year in the Garden

🌸 Spring — February through April

Spring is the prime planting season in Houston. The soil is warming, last frost has typically passed by mid-February, and the summer heat hasn't arrived to stress new transplants. This is your window.

✦ Spring Field Notes

  • Bluebonnets (Texas state flower) bloom March–April along roadsides and in unmowed areas — one of the great landscape spectacles of the Gulf Coast spring
  • Azaleas peak in March — look for them in shaded, east-facing beds where they get morning sun and afternoon protection
  • Knockout Roses begin their season in March and bloom continuously through first frost — the workhorse of Katy landscaping for good reason
  • Warm-season vegetables: Tomatoes, peppers, squash, and beans go in the ground after last frost (typically mid-February in Katy)
  • Lawn care: Time to overseed bare patches and apply pre-emergent for summer weeds before soil temp hits 55°F
  • Fertilizing: Wait until St. Augustine grass shows active green growth before first fertilizer application — don't push dormant grass

☀️ Summer — May through September

Summer in Katy is real. July and August highs routinely reach 95–100°F with heat indices above 105°F. Most planting stops during peak summer; the job shifts to maintenance and survival. Work with the season rather than against it.

✦ Summer Field Notes

  • Crepe myrtles bloom June–August — they are everywhere in Katy because they are perfectly adapted: drought-tolerant, heat-loving, spectacular in flower
  • Lantana, esperanza (Tecoma stans), and salvia are the heat-tolerant perennials that bloom when everything else struggles — plant them and leave them alone
  • Mowing height: St. Augustine grass should be mowed at 3.5–4 inches to shade the soil — never remove more than 1/3 of the blade height in a single mow
  • Watering: Deep, infrequent — 1 inch per week total. Daily shallow watering grows shallow roots. Deep watering twice a week builds drought tolerance
  • Chinch bugs: The #1 summer lawn pest in Houston. They cause brown patches that look like drought stress but don't respond to watering. Key tell: the damage progresses rapidly and occurs in hot, dry, full-sun areas first. If watering doesn't fix a brown patch in 72 hours, check for chinch bugs
  • New planting: Avoid planting new trees and shrubs in peak summer heat. If you must, water twice daily for the first two weeks

🍂 Fall — October through November

Fall is the second great planting season on the Gulf Coast — and in many ways the better one. Soil temperatures have dropped from peak, roots can establish before summer, and the cool-season growing window opens. This is also the best time of year to be outside.

✦ Fall Field Notes

  • Best time to plant trees and shrubs — roots establish through the mild winter and plants hit spring already acclimated to your soil
  • Cool-season vegetables: Broccoli, spinach, kale, carrots, and leafy greens go in the ground in October — one of Houston's underappreciated growing windows
  • Spring bulbs: Plant tulips, daffodils, and hyacinths now for spring color — refrigerate tulips for 6 weeks first (they need the chill)
  • Native plant highlights: Turk's cap (Malvaviscus), fall aster (Symphyotrichum), and goldenrod (Solidago) bloom October–November and are spectacular in naturalistic plantings
  • Bird migration: Painted buntings, warblers, and hummingbirds pass through in October — maintain feeders and native plantings for migrating species. If you have native plants, the birds will find you
  • Last fertilizer: September is the last fertilizer window for St. Augustine — no nitrogen applications after September or you risk tender growth heading into any frost event

❄️ Winter — December through January

Katy winters are mild compared to almost anywhere a relocating family is coming from. Freezes below 28°F happen, but rarely more than a few nights per winter. Many plants stay green. The garden doesn't stop — it pauses.

✦ Winter Field Notes

  • Paperwhites and pansies provide winter color and thrive in Gulf Coast mild winters
  • Live oaks are evergreen — they drop leaves briefly in late February/early March (not in winter) as new growth pushes old leaves off. First-time live oak owners often think their tree is dying. It isn't
  • Crape myrtles: Prune only if structurally necessary. "Crape murder" — the practice of topping crape myrtles — is widespread in Texas and harmful. A properly grown crape myrtle needs no annual pruning. If yours has been topped, resist the temptation to continue
  • Watering: Scale back significantly — most established plants need very little supplemental water in winter. Exception: container plants and newly transplanted material
  • Planning season: Pull the seed catalogs. Draw the bed plans. Order plants for spring. The best landscapes are designed in January

The Dominant Turf

St. Augustine Grass — The Katy Lawn

St. Augustine grass is the lawn turf of greater Houston. You will inherit it, maintain it, and come to understand it over your first summer here. It's a beautiful turf when well cared for — thick, lush, a rich blue-green in peak health. It also has specific requirements that differ from what most transplants know from northern or drier climates.

✦ St. Augustine Care Guide

  • Mowing height: 3.5–4 inches. Never remove more than 1/3 of blade at once — scalping St. Augustine in summer heat can kill sections outright
  • Watering: 1 inch per week, applied in 2 deep sessions rather than 7 shallow ones. Let the soil dry slightly between watering — deep roots handle drought; shallow roots die from it
  • Fertilizing schedule: Apply slow-release nitrogen in May and September. Skip summer peak (July–August) and winter. Never fertilize dormant grass
  • Shade tolerance: Better than most warm-season grasses, but it needs 4–6 hours of direct sun minimum. Shaded areas under maturing trees will thin over time — consider ground covers like mondo grass or Asian jasmine for deep shade
  • Thatch management: Dethatch every 3–4 years if thatch layer exceeds 1/2 inch — excess thatch blocks water and fertilizer from reaching roots
  • Chinch bugs: Most destructive pest. Brown patches in full sun that don't respond to watering. Treat with appropriate insecticide at first sign — they spread rapidly in heat
  • Take-all root rot: A fungal disease that looks like irregular yellowing. Treat with sphagnum peat moss applied to affected areas — it lowers soil pH and suppresses the fungus

Plant Selection

Native Plants for the Katy Landscape

Native plants are not a compromise — they're a performance advantage. Plants evolved in the Gulf Coast region handle heat, clay soil, and drought without the constant inputs that non-native species require. Once established (typically 1–2 seasons), most natives need minimal irrigation, no fertilizer, and minimal maintenance. They also support local pollinators and birds in ways that non-native ornamentals cannot.

Texas Mountain Laurel

Sophora secundiflora. Evergreen shrub/small tree. Stunning purple grape-scented flowers in early spring. Drought-tolerant once established. Slow-growing but worth every year.

Beautyberry

Callicarpa americana. Deciduous shrub with spectacular magenta berry clusters in fall. Birds love it. Grows in sun or partial shade. Cut it hard in late winter — it will reward you with vigorous growth.

Texas Sage (Cenizo)

Leucophyllum frutescens. Silvery-gray evergreen shrub. Erupts in purple flowers in response to high humidity events — often after summer rains. Almost unkillable in full sun well-drained conditions.

Turk's Cap

Malvaviscus arboreus. Loves shade — thrives under trees where almost nothing else grows. Red twisted blooms from summer through fall attract hummingbirds constantly. Extremely tough.

Gulf Coast Muhly Grass

Muhlenbergia capillaris. Warm-season ornamental grass with spectacular pink/purple haze in October–November. Full sun. One of the showiest fall-blooming plants in the Gulf Coast palette.

Salvia greggii

Autumn sage. Red, pink, or coral flowers from spring through frost. Hummingbirds and bees depend on it. Drought-tolerant in well-drained soil. Cut back hard after first flush for continuous bloom.

Inland Sea Oats

Chasmanthium latifolium. The best ornamental grass for shade in Houston. Graceful drooping seed heads. Naturalizes readily under trees where lawn won't grow. Deer-resistant.

Flame Acanthus

Anisacanthus quadrifidus. Orange-red tubular flowers through summer and fall. Hummingbird magnet. Extremely heat and drought tolerant. Looks dead in winter; returns vigorously in spring.

New Homeowner

Your First 90 Days in a New Yard

✦ Before You Touch Anything

The biggest mistake new homeowners make is immediate renovation. Wait one full growing season before making major changes to an established landscape. What looks dead in January may be a spectacular summer perennial. What looks like a shrub in March may be a crape myrtle about to bloom. Observe first.

Watch how water moves across the property in a heavy rain event. Identify any low areas that hold water and determine whether they're drainage features (keep them) or problems (address them before planting). The drainage map of a property is as important as the planting plan.

✦ The 90-Day Checklist

  • Locate and test your irrigation system — identify head types, zones, and any broken heads
  • Get a soil test from the Texas A&M AgriLife Extension Service — know your pH and nutrient levels before adding amendments
  • Walk the property after the first heavy rain and map water flow
  • Identify all existing plants before removing anything — take photos and use an ID app if needed
  • Establish a mowing schedule appropriate for the season — no scalping, no rushed starts
  • Note which areas are in full sun, partial shade, and deep shade — this drives all planting decisions
  • Connect with the Texas A&M AgriLife Extension Fort Bend or Harris County office — free local horticultural expertise
