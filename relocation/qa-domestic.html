Why are so many people moving to Katy and Houston from other states?

A few powerful factors drive Texas migration:

  • No state income tax — Texas has none. For high earners, this is a significant financial shift. Someone earning $200,000 in California paid roughly 9.3% in state income tax. In Texas: zero.
  • Cost of living — Housing, groceries, and services are substantially cheaper than coastal metros. The same dollar buys meaningfully more here.
  • Job market — Energy, healthcare, technology, manufacturing, and financial services all have major Houston presences. ExxonMobil, Shell, BP, Memorial Hermann, Houston Methodist, Hewlett Packard Enterprise, and dozens of other major employers are active.
  • Space — Backyards. Garages. Square footage. Things that cost a fortune in San Jose or Brooklyn are standard here.
  • Business climate — Texas is consistently rated among the most business-friendly states. Low regulation, no corporate income tax, and a legal environment that supports ownership.

What should I understand about Texas property taxes before I move?

This is the most common financial surprise for out-of-state buyers, and I'd rather you hear it from me first than discover it at closing.

Texas has no state income tax — but it has high property taxes. Effective rates in the Katy area typically run 2.3%–3.2% of assessed value, depending on the county, school district, and MUD district. This is meaningfully higher than the national average.

On a $500,000 home, you could be looking at $11,500–$16,000 per year in property taxes. That's real money, and it needs to be factored into your monthly budget alongside your mortgage payment.

✦ Christa's Note

Don't assume low property taxes because you heard there's "no income tax." They are two separate things, and both matter. The Homestead Exemption (below) helps — but it doesn't eliminate the difference. Budget for the real number before you commit to a purchase price.

What is the Texas Homestead Exemption and how do I claim it?

The Homestead Exemption is one of the most valuable benefits of owning a primary residence in Texas. It reduces your taxable assessed value for school district purposes, caps annual increases in your assessed value at 10% per year, and provides additional exemptions for seniors (65+) and disabled veterans.

To claim it: file a Homestead Exemption application with your county appraisal district — FBCAD (fbcad.org) if you're in Fort Bend County, HCAD (hcad.org) if you're in Harris County — by April 30 of the year following your purchase. You must have owned and occupied the home as your principal residence on January 1st of the tax year. Filing is free. Do not pay a third-party service to do this for you — it takes 10 minutes on the appraisal district's website.

I walk every buyer I work with through this process — it's easy to miss if no one mentions it.

How does the cost of living compare to California or the Northeast?

The difference is substantial. Rough order of magnitude:

  • A home that costs $1,200,000 in the Bay Area or Westchester County might cost $450,000–$650,000 in Katy with equivalent or more square footage
  • State income tax savings for a dual-income professional household can be $15,000–$40,000/year or more
  • Utilities are higher in summer (air conditioning is real) but lower in winter vs. northeastern heating costs
  • Groceries, dining, and services are generally 15–25% cheaper than coastal metro areas
  • Auto insurance is often higher in Texas — worth noting when you're running your numbers

The refrain I hear from relocated clients: "I knew it would be cheaper. I didn't realize how much cheaper."

What about the weather? I've heard summers are brutal.

They are. I'm not going to pretend otherwise. June through September means heat indexes of 100–110°F, high humidity, and relatively little relief until you're indoors. Air conditioning is not a luxury here — it's infrastructure. You'll run it from April through October.

The trade-offs: winters are genuinely mild. Highs in the 50s–60s December through February, occasional dips into the 20s–30s (the February 2021 freeze was a historic outlier, not the norm), and almost no snow or ice in normal years. Spring and fall are beautiful.

The honest summary: if you hate summer heat, you'll find Houston summers difficult. If you hate winter cold, you'll love it here. Most relocated clients adapt by staying active outdoors in the early mornings and evenings and accepting that July afternoons are for indoor activities.

How competitive is the market? Will I lose multiple offers?

It depends on price range and current conditions. In the $300,000–$550,000 range in top Katy ISD sections, well-priced homes regularly receive multiple offers within 48–72 hours of listing. Being prepared — pre-approval in hand, clear budget, flexibility to move quickly — is essential.

In the $600,000+ range, the market is more measured. New construction timelines are their own separate dynamic. I'll give you an honest current read when we talk — I'm not interested in manufacturing urgency, but I won't tell you to be slow when speed matters.

Yes — I do this regularly. I provide thorough video walkthroughs via FaceTime or video call, detailed photo documentation, neighborhood context, school campus visits, and honest commentary on condition. I know these communities well enough to tell you what a listing photo isn't showing you. I'll flag deferred maintenance, lot concerns, or neighborhood context that photos won't capture.

What's different about Texas real estate contracts?

Texas uses standardized TREC (Texas Real Estate Commission) promulgated contracts. A few Texas-specific elements that surprise out-of-state buyers:

  • Option Period — A Texas-specific concept. Buyers pay a small negotiable fee (typically $100–$500) for a window of 7–10 days during which they can terminate for any reason and get their earnest money back. This is your due diligence window. Inspections happen here.
  • Earnest Money — Typically 1% of purchase price or a flat negotiated amount, deposited with the title company. At risk if you terminate outside the option period without a contract-approved reason.
  • Title Companies — Texas closings run through title companies, not attorneys. The title company handles escrow, title search, and closing.
  • Closing Timeline — Typically 21–35 days for financed purchases. Cash closes can be faster.
  • No attorney-required closing — Some states require attorneys at closing. Texas generally doesn't.

What should I know about HOAs in Texas?

Texas HOAs have strong legal authority under state law, and they use it. Deed restrictions in master-planned communities are real and enforced: grass height, exterior paint colors, driveway cleanliness, fence types, and more. Fines for violations are legitimate and collectible. An HOA in Texas can even place a lien on your property for unpaid dues.

Before you buy, I pull the HOA documents and walk you through the rules. If you're coming from a state where HOA enforcement was lax, adjust your expectations. Most buyers find it's not onerous — the rules are what maintain community aesthetics and property values — but it's worth knowing upfront.

Which neighborhoods do you recommend for families from California, the Northeast, or Midwest?

My answer is always: tell me where you work, what your budget is, and what your school priorities are — and I'll give you a specific answer. That said, some patterns I've observed:

  • California families moving for Energy Corridor jobs, accustomed to quality suburban living and strong schools: Cinco Ranch, Cross Creek Ranch (Katy ISD sections)
  • Northeast families (NY, NJ, CT, MA) accustomed to walkable town centers and strong schools: Cinco Ranch — LaCenterra is the closest thing to a community "main street" in the area
  • Midwest families (IL, OH, MI) moving for value and space: Katy mid-range communities — Morton Ranch corridor, Pine Mill Ranch, or Cinco Ranch if budget allows

Should I rent first or buy immediately?

Both are valid strategies. Rent first if you're uncertain about your long-term Houston commitment, not ready to navigate the buying process under time pressure, or want to experience different neighborhoods before committing.

Buy immediately if you have a clear destination (specific employer, specific school need), you're financially prepared, and you have the time to do the process properly.

My general take: if you know you're here long-term and can do the work upfront, buying is usually the right call. If you're not sure about the city or the market, a 12-month lease lets you figure it out with lower stakes.
