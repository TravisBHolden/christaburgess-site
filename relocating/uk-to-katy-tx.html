From United Kingdom to Katy, TX

From the UK to Katy.

A well-traveled corridor — Houston has been a posting for British energy, engineering, and finance families for sixty years. The infrastructure for the move is excellent. The climate is the part no one prepares you for.

British families have been relocating to Houston since the 1960s — the oil industry built the corridor, and the schools, parishes, and groceries followed.

The British International School of Houston, The Awty International School, and several established Anglican parishes anchor the community. Phoenicia Specialty Foods carries the tea, Marmite, and HP Sauce. The Houston Cricket League runs spring through autumn. The transition is well-supported — but the climate, the property-tax burden, and the absence of an NHS equivalent are three real adjustments that take a full year. Questions British Families Most Often Ask

Is Katy, Texas a good place for British expats to live?

Yes — the Houston metro has hosted a substantial British community since the 1960s, and Katy and Fulshear are the two suburbs most often chosen by relocating British families, particularly those connected to the energy and engineering industries.

Katy ISD's schools, the proximity to the Energy Corridor along I-10, and the availability of newer construction at prices that feel reasonable by London standards make it the default choice. The British community is well-established: there is a career-level British Consulate-General in Houston, a long-running British American Business Council Houston chapter, the British International School of Houston in Westchase (about 20 minutes from Katy), Anglican parishes including St. Martin's Episcopal in Houston with a strong British community, and a Houston Cricket League that runs from March through October.

How does the climate in Katy, Texas compare to the United Kingdom?

Both technically fall into USDA Hardiness Zone 9a, but the comparison ends there — Houston summers are roughly 12°C warmer than UK summers, with substantially higher humidity, and the rainy season is concentrated in spring and autumn rather than spread across the year.

July and August average 33°C in Houston versus 21°C in southern England. Combined with humidity routinely over 70%, the heat index regularly exceeds 38–42°C. Outdoor activity between 11am and 6pm is genuinely restricted from late May through mid-September. The flip side: Texas winters are mild — December–February temperatures usually 5–18°C — and many British families find that they spend more time outside in January than they did in July.

Rainfall in Houston averages roughly 1,300mm annually (compared to 600–800mm in southern England), but it arrives in concentrated downpours, not the steady drizzle British gardeners know. The implication for gardening is significant: drainage matters more than irrigation, and well-drained soil is the single most important factor in plant survival.

What schools are best for British children moving to Katy?

Three good options: Katy ISD's public schools (highly rated, no tuition, the default for most families), The British International School of Houston (full British curriculum and IB, in Westchase, ~20-min commute), and The Awty International School (French/IB curriculum, ~30-min commute).

Katy ISD includes Cinco Ranch High School, Seven Lakes High School, Tompkins High School, and Jordan High School — all consistently rated among the highest in Texas. British students typically slot in well, with the main academic translation being the move from GCSE/A-Level to the US high school + AP/IB pathway. Counselors at Cinco Ranch and Seven Lakes handle this transition regularly.

BIS Houston runs the National Curriculum for England through Year 13 and offers the IB Diploma in the final years. For families on shorter postings (3–5 years) or wanting to preserve a route back to UK universities, BIS is often the right choice despite the commute. Awty offers a French/IB pathway that is excellent academically but most useful for families with a French connection or genuinely committed to multilingual education.

How does Texas tax compare to UK tax for relocating families?

Texas has no state income tax — only US federal income tax plus property tax — which means a relocating family earning above £100,000 typically pays substantially less total tax than the UK equivalent, though property tax (2.5–3.2% of home value annually) is much higher than UK council tax.

US federal tax has progressive rates from 10% to 37%, generally comparable to UK income tax rates but without National Insurance and without the UK's 60% effective marginal rate band at £100,000–£125,000. The trade-off: property tax in Katy/Fulshear runs roughly $18,000–$22,000 per year on a $700,000 home, dramatically higher than UK council tax on an equivalent property.

British families need to plan around three specific issues: (1) the US–UK tax treaty and tie-breaker rules during the transition year, (2) UK ISAs and SIPPs may be treated as taxable in the US, (3) the IRS's reporting requirements on UK assets (Form 8938, FBAR). Christa connects clients with cross-border tax advisors experienced with US–UK relocation before any property transaction.

Can I drive in Texas with my UK driver's license?

Yes for up to 90 days after establishing Texas residency — after that you must obtain a Texas driver's license, which for UK license holders typically requires the written test but not the road test.

Documents required at the Texas Department of Public Safety: valid UK passport, US visa, Social Security number (or letter of ineligibility), two proofs of Texas residency, and the original UK driver's license. The written test is straightforward; most UK license holders pass on the first attempt.

Driving culture is a real adjustment — automatic transmissions are universal, lanes are wider, US highway speeds are higher, and the absence of roundabouts is surprisingly disorienting. The good news for UK arrivals: petrol is roughly 30% of UK prices, and the secondary used-car market is deep. Most families end up with two vehicles; the Houston metro is not walkable in the UK sense, and Katy in particular is built for cars.

Where can British families find community in the Houston area?

Three anchor institutions: the British Consulate-General Houston, the British American Business Council Houston, and St. Martin's Episcopal Church (Memorial area) — joined by the British International School community, the Houston Cricket League, and informal expat networks.

The Consulate-General hosts an annual Queen's Birthday (now King's Birthday) party that effectively functions as the British community's annual reunion. The British American Business Council runs monthly networking events. St. Martin's Episcopal in Memorial holds a service that draws heavily from the British Anglican community, with a number of clergy who have served in the Church of England.

Beyond formal institutions: the BIS Houston parent body is the densest concentration of British families in the city, and the Houston Cricket League (HCL) provides Saturday social structure from March through October. Christa maintains a current contact list of British families who have settled in Katy over the past five years; the most useful onboarding resource is typically the family who arrived two years ago.

Where can I buy British food and groceries in the Houston area?

Phoenicia Specialty Foods (downtown Houston and Westheimer locations) is the primary source — they carry PG Tips, Yorkshire Tea, HP Sauce, Branston Pickle, Marmite, Heinz baked beans (UK formulation), Cadbury chocolate, and Walker's crisps consistently.

Beyond Phoenicia: Whole Foods and Central Market carry a respectable British section. H-E-B's flagship stores carry surprising amounts (PG Tips in particular). For products not stocked locally, British Corner Shop and TheBritishFoodDepot ship to Texas reliably. Costco Houston carries Walker's shortbread and McVitie's digestives in bulk.

Christmas is the season most British families miss home — the pudding, the mince pies, the cracker tradition. Phoenicia stocks Christmas pudding from mid-November. Crackers can be ordered direct. The Houston King's Head Pub (Galleria area) and a handful of other British pubs run a proper Christmas meal that helps soften the first holiday season abroad.

What plants from my UK garden will grow in Katy, Texas?

Some — but with significant caveats: roses, lavender, rosemary, and most Mediterranean herbs translate well, while delphiniums, lupins, peonies, foxgloves, and most British cottage-garden staples will not survive a Houston summer.

Roses are the great translation. The David Austin English Rose cultivars 'Munstead Wood', 'Lady of Shalott', 'Olivia Rose Austin', and 'The Generous Gardener' all perform well in Zone 9a with morning sun and afternoon shade. Lavender (the Mediterranean cultivars, not English lavender) does well in raised beds with sharp drainage.

What you will lose: the cottage garden as a concept. Most British perennials simply cannot survive Texas July and August. What you will gain: figs (productive within two seasons), pomegranates, satsumas, Meyer lemons, a year-round herb garden that includes basil, and the entire universe of Texas native wildflowers — bluebonnets, Indian paintbrush, gaillardia, Mexican feathergrass. Christa, Texas A&M-trained in horticulture, walks the property with British arrivals in the first season and recommends a 'translation list' of plant substitutions.

What is healthcare like for British families moving to Katy?

There is no NHS equivalent — US healthcare runs on private insurance (typically employer-provided for relocating families), with co-pays, deductibles, and provider networks that all become live financial decisions in ways they are not in the UK.

For families relocating with an employer: the employer's health plan typically provides comprehensive coverage, and the Houston Methodist, Memorial Hermann, MD Anderson, and Texas Medical Center networks are among the best in the United States. For specialist care, the Texas Medical Center is the largest medical complex in the world and is 40–60 minutes from Katy.

Two adjustments to expect: (1) the cost transparency is poor; the same procedure can cost dramatically different amounts at different providers, and asking for an estimate is normal practice; (2) preventive care is well covered but most US plans push patients toward in-network providers, which restricts choice in ways the NHS does not. The flip side: wait times for specialists are typically 1–3 weeks, not 1–3 months. Christa connects new arrivals with British families who have navigated their employer's specific health plan.

What surprises British families most about life in Katy, Texas?

Three things, in this order: the size of everything (houses, supermarkets, road lanes), how genuinely friendly strangers are (Texan small talk is not transactional), and the heat in July and August.

Most British families adjust to the first two within months and to the third within a full summer. Subtler adjustments: the centrality of the car, the privacy of social life (most entertaining happens in private homes, not pubs), the absence of decent tea in restaurants, and the genuine difficulty of finding good fish and chips. The Houston King's Head Pub does a respectable version; most families learn to make their own.

The pleasant surprises: how good Katy ISD's public schools are, how affordable groceries are (Costco in particular is a revelation), how mild the winters feel, and how strongly the British community supports new arrivals. The British International School parent body and the Houston Cricket League both function as informal welcome committees. Christa has guided more than thirty British families through this transition and maintains an introduction list of families who arrived in the previous three years.

Naturalist's Note

An English rose garden translates to Texas — but not the one you would plant in the Cotswolds. The David Austin program (formally Austin Roses, founded in Shropshire in the 1960s) has spent four decades breeding for repeat-flowering, disease resistance, and heat tolerance. Several of their cultivars — 'Munstead Wood', 'Lady of Shalott', 'Olivia Rose Austin', 'The Generous Gardener' — perform genuinely well in Zone 9a if given morning sun, afternoon shade, and the same compost generosity they would receive at home. The fragrance, mercifully, transports intact.

Christa's Tip

Plan your scouting trip for the second week of November. Houston is at its absolute best then — daytime temperatures around 18–22°C, the bluestem and switchgrass in the Cross Creek Ranch prairie restoration in full bronze color, low humidity, and the Christmas markets at LaCenterra just opening. The combination tells a British family the truth about Houston in the season Houston shows itself best, while letting them assess the housing stock at a measured pace.
