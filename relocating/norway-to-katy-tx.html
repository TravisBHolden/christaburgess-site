From Norway to Katy, TX

From Norway to Katy.

Stavanger has been Houston's sister city since 1980. The path between them is well-worn — but the climate, the soil, and the rhythm of a day all reset on arrival.

More Norwegian families relocate to the Houston metro than to any other US region — drawn by the energy industry, kept by the schools and the cost of living.

Equinor, Aker Solutions, DNV, TGS, Subsea7, and a dozen smaller Norwegian engineering firms maintain major offices in Houston's Energy Corridor and the surrounding business parks. Many of their employees settle in Katy and Fulshear. The transition is not trivial — the climate, the housing typology, and the tax structure are all genuinely different — but the path is established. This guide answers the questions Norwegian families have asked Christa across two decades of relocations.

Questions Norwegian Families Most Often Ask

Why do so many Norwegians live in the Houston area?

Because the Norwegian energy industry is concentrated there — Equinor, Aker Solutions, DNV, TGS, Subsea7, and a long list of supporting firms all maintain major Houston offices.

The Stavanger–Houston sister-city relationship, formalized in 1980, anchors the connection. Beyond the energy sector, the Houston metro hosts the Honorary Consulate of Norway, the Norwegian Seamen's Church (Sjømannskirken), Sons of Norway Lodge Nordkap, and an established Norwegian-American community that gathers around 17. mai (Constitution Day) and Sankthans every year. For a Norwegian family with school-aged children, Katy and Fulshear are the two suburbs most often chosen — Katy ISD's strong international population and Lamar CISD's newer campuses make adjustment unusually smooth.

How different is the climate in Katy, Texas from Norway?

Profoundly — Katy sits in USDA Hardiness Zone 9a (winter lows around 20–25°F / −4 to −7°C), while most of populated Norway sits in Zones 4–7 with winter lows from −10 to −30°C. Summer is the bigger shock: Houston averages 33°C in July versus Oslo's 17°C.

The transition takes about two summers. The first summer feels oppressive — outdoor activity is genuinely limited between 11am and 6pm from late May through September. The second summer feels normal. Air conditioning is not a luxury here; it is the equivalent of central heating in Bergen — non-negotiable infrastructure that runs roughly six months a year. Norwegian habits that translate well include early-morning outdoor time (cooler, lighter mosquito pressure), the Norwegian appetite for walks (the Cinco Ranch trail system is genuinely walkable in spring and fall), and shoulder-season travel (March and October are spectacular in Texas).

Winter is the pleasant surprise. December–February are mild — temperatures usually 5–18°C — and many Norwegian families find themselves spending more time outside in January than they did in July.

What schools are best for Norwegian children moving to Katy?

Katy ISD's seven on-site Cinco Ranch campuses and Seven Lakes High School all accommodate international students with established ESL and counselor-led integration programs; for families wanting a continued international curriculum, The Awty International School (Houston) offers French and IB programs, and the British International School of Houston serves the IB and Cambridge tracks.

Norwegian children typically slot into the standard Katy ISD pathway and adjust quickly — English fluency is usually strong, and the Norwegian academic foundation tends to be at or ahead of grade level in mathematics and reading. The cultural adjustment is the real work. Katy ISD's counselors actively manage the first 90 days for international students. Families who want a smaller, IB-curriculum environment more reminiscent of European schooling typically look at Awty (closer to downtown, ~30-minute commute from Katy) or BIS Houston (Westchase, ~20 minutes from Katy).

There is no Norwegian-language school in Houston; supplemental Norwegian language and culture instruction is provided informally through Sjømannskirken and through the Norwegian Society of Texas's youth programming.

How does Texas tax compare to Norwegian tax for a relocating family?

Texas has no state income tax — only federal income tax plus property tax, which means a family earning $200,000 typically pays substantially less total tax than the same family in Norway, though the structure is different (high property tax, no universal healthcare cost-shifted into payroll).

The federal tax structure is progressive with marginal rates from 10% to 37%. There is no equivalent to the Norwegian formuesskatt (wealth tax) at the federal level, and no state income tax in Texas at all. The trade-off: property tax in the Katy/Fulshear area runs 2.5–3.2% of assessed home value annually (combining school district, county, and MUD components), substantially higher than Norwegian eiendomsskatt. On a $700,000 home, that is roughly $18,000–$22,000 per year.

Healthcare is the other major shift. There is no universal coverage equivalent to HELFO; most Norwegian employees relocating with their company will be enrolled in the employer's group health plan, but co-pays, deductibles, and provider networks all become live financial decisions in a way they are not in Norway. Christa connects relocating families with cross-border tax advisors familiar with the US-Norway tax treaty (1971, amended) and the FATCA reporting obligations that apply to Norwegian financial accounts held by US residents.

Can I drive in Texas with my Norwegian driver's license?

Yes, for up to 90 days from the date you establish Texas residency — after that you must obtain a Texas driver's license, which for Norwegians typically requires the written test but not the road test (Texas accepts the Norwegian license as evidence of competency).

Documents required at the Texas Department of Public Safety (TX DPS): valid Norwegian passport, US visa or green card, Social Security number or letter of ineligibility, two proofs of Texas residency (lease, utility bill, etc.), and the original Norwegian driver's license. A certified translation of the Norwegian license is recommended though not always required. The written test is in English and is straightforward — most relocating Norwegians pass on the first attempt.

Vehicle culture is a real adjustment. The Houston metro is built for cars; the Katy and Fulshear suburbs are not walkable in the Norwegian sense. Most families end up with two vehicles. The good news for Norwegians: gasoline is roughly 25–30% of Norwegian prices, and the secondary used-car market is deep and reliable.

Where can Norwegian families find community in the Houston area?

Three institutions anchor the Norwegian-American community in Houston: the Honorary Consulate of Norway, the Norwegian Seamen's Church (Sjømannskirken Houston), and Sons of Norway Lodge Nordkap.

Sjømannskirken Houston is the primary cultural and pastoral anchor — Norwegian-language services, the annual Christmas julemesse, the 17. mai breakfast, and a pre-school program for Norwegian-speaking children. The church operates from a Houston-area facility and runs programs across the metro. Sons of Norway Lodge Nordkap holds monthly gatherings with traditional Norwegian food, lectures, and cultural programming.

Beyond formal organizations: the Norwegian Society of Texas maintains a mailing list and a Facebook group through which new arrivals find informal mentorship — which family lives in which subdivision, which English-as-a-second-language tutor works well for teenagers, which Norwegian grocery shipments arrive at which port. Christa keeps a current contact list and makes introductions for relocating families on request.

What Norwegian groceries and food can I find in the Houston area?

The IKEA Houston Food Market (Houston-Northwest) carries lingonberry preserves, knäckebröd, salted licorice, Kalles kaviar, Swedish meatballs, and several other Scandinavian staples; Phoenicia Specialty Foods (downtown Houston and Westheimer) carries Jarlsberg, brown cheese (Brunost) on order, and European fish products.

For Norwegian-specific items not stocked locally, two practical paths: Sjømannskirken occasionally coordinates bulk shipments of Norwegian products for the holiday season (julebrus, lutefisk for those who want it, traditional cookies); and Amazon's international grocery section now ships a surprising amount of Norwegian product directly. Costco Houston carries excellent Norwegian salmon year-round at prices Norwegians find remarkable.

For fresh seafood, H-E-B's flagship Cinco Ranch store (23119 Cinco Ranch Blvd) carries Norwegian Atlantic salmon and Faroese halibut consistently. Central Market and Whole Foods both have strong fish counters as well. The Saturday Farmers Market at Fulshear City Hall hosts a Gulf shrimp vendor that becomes most Norwegian families' summer fish habit.

What plants from my Norwegian garden will survive in Katy, Texas?

Very few — Norway and Texas Hill Country/Coastal Plain are gardening on opposite ends of the climate spectrum. Birch, spruce, blueberries, lingonberries, peonies, lily-of-the-valley, and most Norwegian alpines will not survive a Houston summer.

Plants that translate: rosemary (perennial here, dies back in Norway), lavender (the Mediterranean cultivars), sage, thyme, oregano, mint (be careful — it spreads aggressively in Zone 9a), strawberries (early-spring crop, not summer), and rhubarb (with some shade work). Tulips work if treated as annuals; they will not naturalize.

Plants that will delight a Norwegian gardener with their novelty: figs (productive within two seasons), pomegranates, satsuma mandarins, Meyer lemons (with winter protection), bougainvillea (a tropical that overwinters in protected spots), and the entire universe of native Texas wildflowers — bluebonnets in March, Indian paintbrush in April, gaillardia all summer. Christa, a Texas A&M-trained horticulturist, will walk the property with you in the first season and identify which existing plantings are worth keeping and which to replace.

Where do Norwegian families worship in the Houston area?

Sjømannskirken Houston offers regular Norwegian-language Lutheran services; Salem Lutheran Church (Tomball), Houston Christian Lutheran (Sugar Land), and several ELCA-affiliated congregations in the Katy area welcome Norwegian-Americans into English-language Lutheran worship.

For Norwegian families accustomed to Den norske kirke (Church of Norway), the closest doctrinal and liturgical equivalent in the United States is the Evangelical Lutheran Church in America (ELCA). The Houston metro has a dense ELCA presence. Catholic families coming from the small Norwegian Catholic community will find Holy Rosary, St. Bartholomew, and Epiphany of the Lord in Katy as well-attended Catholic parishes with welcoming international ministries.

Sjømannskirken remains the cultural and linguistic anchor for most relocating Norwegian families during the first year, even those who don't typically attend church in Norway — the social network, the language, the seasonal liturgical rhythm all matter more after a long move than they did at home.

What about hiking, skiing, and outdoor life — the friluftsliv question?

Skiing is essentially gone — the closest meaningful slopes are in New Mexico, a 14-hour drive — but the Texas friluftsliv reframes around water and birds rather than snow and mountains.

Brazos Bend State Park (40 minutes south of Katy) offers alligator-viewing trails, the George Observatory for astronomy nights, and the closest meaningful wilderness to the metro. Memorial Park and George Bush Park, both within 30 minutes, offer trail running and cycling. Galveston Bay sailing, kayaking in Buffalo Bayou, and wake-boarding on the area lakes become the warm-weather equivalents of cross-country skiing.

Norwegian families typically rediscover bird-watching here — the Katy Prairie is on the eastern edge of the Central Flyway, one of North America's great migratory corridors. Spring and fall migrations bring painted buntings, scissor-tailed flycatchers, and roseate spoonbills within an easy weekend drive. The Norwegian instinct toward unstructured outdoor time translates better than most expats expect.

What surprises Norwegian families most about life in Katy, Texas?

Three things, in roughly this order: the sheer size of everything (houses, roads, parking lots, grocery stores), the friendliness of strangers (Texan small talk is a genuine custom, not a transaction), and the heat in August.

Most Norwegian families adjust to the first two within months and to the third within two summers. The slower adjustments tend to be subtler: the absence of universal public infrastructure (no national rail, sparse public transit, very limited sidewalk culture), the centrality of the car, and the fact that most social life happens in private homes and private clubs rather than in public spaces. Norwegian families who choose Cinco Ranch or Cross Creek Ranch often cite the strength of the trail systems and the walkability of LaCenterra as the closest available analog to a Norwegian neighborhood.

The pleasant surprises: how good the public schools are, how mild the winters feel, how varied the wildlife is, and how strongly Houston's international community supports new arrivals. Christa has guided more than two dozen Norwegian families through this transition and maintains an introduction list of families who arrived in the previous three years — the single best onboarding resource is the family who finished the same move two years ago.

Naturalist's Note

The Norwegian lupine (Lupinus polyphyllus), so beloved in roadside ditches from Lofoten to Trondheim, is a cousin of the Texas state flower — Lupinus texensis, the bluebonnet. Different species, same genus; both prefer poor soil, both fix nitrogen, both refuse to grow where they are coddled. A relocating Norwegian gardener should arrive in October with a packet of bluebonnet seed, scatter it on the driest, sunniest corner of the new yard, and forget about it. By March it will look like a small Texas sky has fallen at your feet — and feel, for a moment, like a Lofoten summer translated into another language.

Christa's Tip

Plan a two-week visit in late February or early March before committing to a neighborhood. Two reasons: first, the climate is genuinely tolerable then (highs 18–22°C, dry, beautiful light), which lets a Norwegian family assess Katy on its merits rather than recoiling from August humidity. Second, the bluebonnets are out, which means you can see which lots and which streets have the best southern exposure — the same exposure that will run your kitchen garden and shade your house in July. Christa coordinates these scouting weeks for Norwegian families regularly.
