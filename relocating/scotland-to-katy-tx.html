Questions Scottish Families Most Often Ask

Is Katy, Texas a good place for Scottish families to live?

Yes — the Aberdeen–Houston energy corridor has brought Scottish families to the Houston metro continuously since the 1960s, and Katy and Fulshear are the two suburbs most often chosen by relocating Scottish engineers, geologists, and energy executives.

The Scottish community in Houston is small relative to other expat groups but unusually well-organized. The St. Andrew's Society of Houston, founded in 1907, runs the annual Burns Night Supper (25 January), the Kirkin' o' the Tartan (April), and the Houston Highland Games (held annually in Humble, just north of Houston). The Caledonian Society of Houston and several pipe bands round out the cultural infrastructure. Many Scottish families also find community through the broader British and BIS Houston networks.

How does the climate in Katy, Texas compare to Scotland?

The two are profoundly different — Houston summers average 33°C versus Scotland's 16°C, and Houston receives roughly twice the annual rainfall, concentrated in spring and autumn rather than spread across the year.

The Scottish climate is among the most consistent in the world: cool, damp, gentle, with limited extremes. Houston is the opposite: a tropical-influenced climate with explosive summer heat, occasional severe weather, and a true winter that lasts only six to eight weeks. Most Scottish families take two summers to fully adjust; the second one feels normal.

The compensating gift: Texas winter. December through February average 5–18°C, often clear and sunny. Many Scottish families discover they spend more time outside in January in Texas than they did in July in Scotland. The trail systems in Cinco Ranch and Cross Creek Ranch become genuinely usable in the shoulder seasons.

What schools are best for Scottish children moving to Katy?

Katy ISD's public schools (free, highly rated, the default), The British International School of Houston (full UK curriculum, ~20-minute commute from Katy), and The Awty International School (French/IB curriculum, ~30 minutes) — most Scottish families choose Katy ISD, with BIS the most common alternative.

Scottish secondary qualifications (National 5, Higher, Advanced Higher) do not have a direct US equivalent, but Katy ISD counselors at Cinco Ranch High, Seven Lakes High, Tompkins High, and Jordan High handle the conversion routinely. Scottish university entry standards remain compatible with US high-school transcripts plus SAT/ACT scores for students returning to Scotland for university.

Families on shorter Aberdeen postings (3–5 years) sometimes choose BIS Houston specifically to keep continuity with the UK academic pathway. Christa connects relocating families with both Katy ISD and BIS admissions officers in the first conversation.

How does Texas tax compare to Scottish tax for relocating families?

Texas has no state income tax — only US federal income tax plus property tax — which for most Scottish families relocating at energy-sector salaries results in materially lower total tax than Scotland, though property tax is much higher than UK council tax.

Scottish income tax now diverges meaningfully from the rest of the UK at higher income bands (the 'top rate' at 48% above £125,140). US federal tax tops at 37%, and Texas adds 0% state income tax. The net effect for an energy-sector family earning above £100,000 is typically significant savings — partially offset by property tax of 2.5–3.2% of home value annually (roughly $18,000–$22,000 on a $700,000 home).

Three Scottish-specific issues to plan around: (1) the US–UK tax treaty applies to Scottish residents and is workable but requires careful planning in the transition year; (2) Scottish workplace pensions and SIPPs may be treated as taxable in the US; (3) FATCA reporting on Scottish bank accounts is mandatory for US tax residents. Christa connects clients with cross-border tax advisors before any property transaction.

Where can Scottish families find community in the Houston area?

The St. Andrew's Society of Houston (founded 1907) is the primary anchor — running Burns Night, the Kirkin' o' the Tartan, and St. Andrew's Day each year — joined by the Houston Highland Games (Humble, annually), the Caledonian Society of Houston, and several active pipe bands.

The Burns Night Supper, traditionally held on or near 25 January, is the most-attended Scottish community event of the year — a full Burns Supper with haggis, neeps and tatties, the Address to a Haggis recited in full Scots, and a ceilidh that runs late. The Kirkin' o' the Tartan (April, at one of the Houston-area churches with Presbyterian connections) blesses the tartans and is the gentler annual highlight.

The Houston Highland Games, held in Humble each year, draw Scottish families from across the metro for caber tossing, pipe band competition, Highland dancing, and a substantial number of Scottish food vendors. For Scottish families with school-aged children, attending the first Highland Games of their tenure is the single most useful community introduction. Christa maintains a current Scottish-family contact list.

Where can I buy Scottish food and groceries in the Houston area?

Phoenicia Specialty Foods carries Tunnock's tea cakes, shortbread (Walker's and Dean's), Irn-Bru, square Lorne sausage on order, and most British items that Scottish kitchens use — while Costco Houston carries good Scottish smoked salmon and Walker's biscuits in bulk.

For products not stocked locally: TheBritishFoodDepot and British Corner Shop ship to Texas reliably, including Scotch broth mix, oatcakes, and the cakes and biscuits Scottish families miss most. Square Lorne sausage and proper black pudding can be ordered through several specialty butchers; Christa keeps a current list.

Whisky: the Texas market for Scotch whisky is excellent. Spec's, Total Wine, and several specialty whisky bars in the Houston Heights stock single malts at prices typically lower than UK retail. The Scottish Whisky Society of Houston meets quarterly.

What plants from my Scottish garden will grow in Katy, Texas?

Very few of the cottage-garden staples — but the live oak (Quercus virginiana) that defines Cinco Ranch's mature canopy is the same species grown at Crarae Garden in Argyll and at Inverewe in Wester Ross.

Scotland's plant palette is built around cool, damp, gentle conditions. Most of the staples — rhododendrons, azaleas, foxgloves, lupins, delphiniums, primulas, ferns — will struggle or fail outright in Houston's summer. The exceptions: rosemary, the Mediterranean lavenders, and rosemary translate well; raspberry canes can produce one spring crop before heat shutdown; rhubarb survives with afternoon shade.

What you gain: the entire warm-climate palette. Figs produce within two seasons. Pomegranates thrive. Citrus (satsuma, Meyer lemon) is a backyard staple. And the live oak — Quercus virginiana — the same evergreen oak Sir Joseph Hooker introduced to the Scottish gardens at Inverewe in the late 19th century, now defines Cinco Ranch's mature canopy. A Scottish gardener arriving in Houston has, in a small way, traded one familiar tree for the same tree in a different sky.

Can I drive in Texas with my Scottish driver's license?

Yes for up to 90 days from establishing Texas residency — after that the Texas driver's license is mandatory, but Scottish licenses are accepted as proof of competency and the road test is typically waived.

Documents at the Texas Department of Public Safety: UK passport with valid US visa, Social Security number (or letter of ineligibility), two proofs of Texas residency, and the original Scottish (UK) driver's license. The written test in English is straightforward.

Practical reality: Houston is a car city in ways Edinburgh and Glasgow are not. Most relocating families end up with two vehicles. Highway driving feels fast, lanes feel wide, and the absence of roundabouts is the most-cited adjustment. The good news: petrol is roughly 30% of UK prices, the secondary used-car market is deep, and tolls on the Westpark Tollway are inexpensive by UK standards.

What is healthcare like for Scottish families moving to Katy?

There is no NHS Scotland equivalent — US healthcare runs on private insurance (typically employer-provided for relocating families), with co-pays, deductibles, and provider networks that all become live financial decisions.

For families relocating with an employer: the employer's health plan typically provides comprehensive coverage. Houston Methodist, Memorial Hermann, MD Anderson, and Texas Medical Center are among the strongest health networks in the United States. The Texas Medical Center is 40–60 minutes from Katy.

Two adjustments: cost transparency is poor (the same procedure can cost dramatically different amounts at different providers); and most US plans push patients toward in-network providers, restricting specialist choice in ways NHS Scotland did not. The flip side: specialist wait times are typically 1–3 weeks, not 1–3 months. Christa connects new arrivals with Scottish families who have navigated their employer's specific plan.

What surprises Scottish families most about life in Katy, Texas?

The heat in July and August, the friendliness of strangers (Texan small talk is not transactional, which Scots particularly appreciate), and the size of the houses and the road lanes.

Most Scottish families adjust to the first within two summers, to the second within weeks, and to the third — frankly — never quite stop being amused by it. Subtler adjustments include the absence of pubs in the Scottish sense (most US 'pubs' do not function as the daily social institution Scottish villages and Edinburgh neighborhoods rely on), the limited public transit, and the absence of decent black pudding outside of specialty butchers.

Pleasant surprises: Katy ISD's strength, the quality of Texan beef and seafood, the genuine warmth of the Scottish-American community at events like Burns Night, and the discovery that the live oaks here are botanically the same species as the ones at Crarae and Inverewe. The Scottish instinct toward unstructured outdoor time translates better than expected — through hiking, sailing on Galveston Bay, and birding the Katy Prairie during the autumn migration.

Naturalist's Note

The live oak (Quercus virginiana), which defines Cinco Ranch's mature canopy and shades half the homes in Katy, is the same species grown in the great Scottish coastal gardens at Crarae (Argyll) and Inverewe (Wester Ross) — both of which inherited mature specimens from the Victorian and Edwardian collectors who brought American oaks to the West Highlands. A Scottish gardener arriving in Houston has, in a small and unexpected way, traded one familiar evergreen tree for the same tree in a different sky. The Aggies, for their part, would point out that the most famous live oak in Texas is the Century Tree on the Texas A&M campus in College Station, where students by tradition do not walk beneath it before graduation.

Christa's Tip

If your move falls in early autumn, time the trip to coincide with the Houston Highland Games in Humble. Beyond the obvious cultural pleasure — caber, pipes, dancing, food — the Games are where every Scottish family in greater Houston is in one place on the same Saturday. The introductions you make there over a single afternoon save you six months of community-finding the slow way. Christa attends most years and is happy to make the introductions personally.
